# AIism catalog

Research reviewed on 2026-07-22. This catalog targets contemporary English prose. The evidence base is strongest for English and varies by model, prompt, genre, and year.

## How to use the catalog

Apply two levels of enforcement:

- **Core formulas**: rewrite by default because the structure usually substitutes performance for thought. Keep only when it is quoted, technically required, or clearly deliberate and effective.
- **Contextual signals**: inspect density, repetition, precision, genre, and voice. A single word, list, or punctuation mark is not a defect. Change it only when it weakens the passage.

Do not use this catalog to decide whether a person used AI. Many patterns originated in human prose, and style alone is unreliable. The editorial goal is better writing, not concealed provenance.

## Core formulas

### Contrastive negation

Watch for:

- `it is not just X; it is Y`
- `not only X, but also Y`
- `not X, but Y`
- `no X, no Y, just Z`
- `X rather than Y` when no real comparison is being argued
- repeated `This isn't... This is...` or `Not because... Because...` fragments

State the positive claim directly or explain the actual relationship.

> Before: The release is not just a redesign; it is a reimagining of collaboration.
>
> After: The release adds shared editing, review queues, and access controls.

Keep contrast when the rejected interpretation is real and relevant. Name who holds it or show why readers might reasonably expect it.

### Staged questions and answer fragments

Watch for `And the result?`, `The kicker?`, `The best part?`, `What does this mean?`, `Why does this matter?`, and questions immediately answered by the writer.

Replace manufactured suspense with the answer. Keep a question only when the piece genuinely examines it or the audience must answer it.

> Before: The result? Teams can move faster than ever before.
>
> After: The new cache cut median build time from nine minutes to four.

### Generic and self-narrating openings

Remove scene-setting that could introduce almost any topic:

- `in today's world` or `in today's fast-paced landscape`
- `when it comes to`
- `in an era where`
- `X is defined as Y, encompassing...` when the audience knows X
- `in this article, we will explore`
- `let's dive into`, `let's unpack`, or `let's take a closer look`

Start with the claim, event, problem, observation, or concrete detail that caused the piece to exist.

> Before: In today's rapidly evolving digital landscape, observability is more important than ever.
>
> After: Last month's outage lasted 47 minutes because the team could not trace requests across services.

### Mechanical symmetry and automatic triads

Watch for repeated three-item lists, three adjectives, three parallel clauses, equal-length paragraphs, and serial `Whether X, Y, or Z` constructions. Count the real items. Use one, two, four, or more when the material supports that number.

> Before: The tool is fast, flexible, and future-ready.
>
> After: The tool processes the current dataset in 12 seconds and accepts custom parsers.

Keep genuine three-part taxonomies, required three-step procedures, and memorable triads that earn their shape.

### Superficial analysis tails

Remove present-participle clauses that merely inflate the preceding fact:

- `highlighting...`
- `underscoring...`
- `showcasing...`
- `reflecting...`
- `contributing to...`
- `fostering...`
- `ensuring...`

Either supply a specific consequence with evidence or stop after the fact.

> Before: The station has six platforms, facilitating the efficient movement of passengers.
>
> After: The station has six platforms and handled 18,400 passengers per weekday in 2025.

### Unearned importance, legacy, and scale

Remove claims that a routine fact `marks a pivotal moment`, `represents a significant shift`, `reflects a broader movement`, `leaves an indelible mark`, `sets the stage`, or `shapes the evolving landscape` unless the passage proves that claim.

Prefer the event and its measured consequence. If significance is disputed or interpretive, attribute the judgment.

> Before: The office opening marked a pivotal moment in the company's global journey.
>
> After: The Dublin office gave the company its first support team in the EU time zones.

### Vague authority and inflated consensus

Watch for `experts argue`, `researchers note`, `industry reports suggest`, `critics contend`, `observers say`, `widely regarded`, and plural attribution supported by one source.

Name the person, study, report, or institution and state what it found. Match singular and plural claims to the evidence. Cut the claim when no source is available.

> Before: Experts widely agree that the policy will transform the industry.
>
> After: In its June forecast, ERCOT estimated that the rule would add 3.2 GW of dispatchable capacity by 2029.

### Speculation after an ignorance disclaimer

Do not pair `details are limited`, `not widely documented`, `based on available information`, or `likely` with invented explanation. State the limit cleanly and stop, or label a supported inference and show its basis.

> Before: While records are scarce, the festival likely played a vital role in community cohesion.
>
> After: No attendance records or contemporary accounts of the festival have been found.

### Formulaic sections and endings

Watch for:

- `Despite its success, X faces several challenges`
- headings such as `Challenges and Future Prospects`, `Future Outlook`, and generic `Conclusion`
- paragraph-level restatement beginning `In summary`, `In conclusion`, `Overall`, or `Taken together`
- vague optimism such as `As X continues to evolve, its potential remains limitless`
- endings that resolve every tension or introduce a call to action the piece has not earned

Discuss limitations where they arise. End on the last useful fact, the actual decision, a supported implication, or a genuine open question.

### Chatbot residue and automatic praise

Remove interface language from finished copy: `Certainly`, `Of course`, `Great question`, `You're absolutely right`, `I hope this helps`, `let me know`, `would you like me to`, and offers to expand or continue.

Do not flatter the writer, reader, or subject. Demonstrate importance with evidence.

### Performed candor and intimacy

Watch for `here's the thing`, `let's be honest`, `the truth is`, `full transparency`, `nobody talks about this`, `genuine question`, and `unpopular opinion` when followed by an ordinary claim.

State the claim. Keep the frame only when the disclosure, disagreement, or surprise is real.

### False balance and frictionless optimism

Do not default to equal `on one hand/on the other hand` treatment when evidence is lopsided. Do not assume universal cooperation, effortless adoption, or positive outcomes. Describe the actual weight of evidence, incentives, costs, dissent, and operational friction.

## Contextual signals

### Vocabulary clusters

Words become signals through frequency, figurative use, and clustering. Do not run a thesaurus replacement pass.

Current GPT-family tendencies reported from mid-2025 onward include `emphasizing`, `enhance`, `highlighting`, and `showcasing`, plus canned claims about notability, attribution, and media coverage. Older but still recognizable clusters include:

- `additionally`, `moreover`, `furthermore`
- `delve`, `unpack`, `navigate`, `leverage`, `utilize`, `facilitate`, `unlock`
- `crucial`, `pivotal`, `robust`, `seamless`, `transformative`, `multifaceted`
- figurative `landscape`, `tapestry`, `journey`, `ecosystem`, `testament`
- `intricate`, `meticulous`, `vibrant`, `profound`, `groundbreaking`, `enduring`
- `actionable insights`, `commitment to excellence`, `rich history`, `diverse array`

Keep a word when it is the precise term. Replace decorative vocabulary with the specific action, property, or consequence.

### Avoidance of `is`, `are`, and `has`

Prefer the basic copula or possession verb when it is accurate. Inspect `serves as`, `stands as`, `functions as`, `marks`, `represents`, `boasts`, `features`, `maintains`, and `offers`.

> Before: Gallery 825 serves as the association's exhibition space.
>
> After: Gallery 825 is the association's exhibition space.

Keep the richer verb when it adds meaning: a hotel can offer breakfast, and a component can function as a circuit breaker.

### Transition filler

Inspect `with this in mind`, `building on this`, `that said`, `having said that`, `in light of this`, `at its core`, and `it is important to note`. Delete them when paragraph order already carries the relationship. Replace them with an exact logical connector such as `because`, `although`, or `therefore` only when that relationship is true.

### Em dashes and dramatic punctuation

An em dash is legitimate punctuation. Change it when it repeatedly connects loosely related clauses, manufactures drama, substitutes for a logical relationship, or appears in a repeated contrast formula. Keep it when an interruption or emphatic parenthesis is intentional and fits the writer's style.

### Formatting defaults

Question formatting that arrives without a content need:

- title-case headings
- many short sections
- bold emphasis on ordinary phrases
- bullets with a bold label, colon, and uniform explanation
- emoji decorating headings or bullets
- tiny tables that prose could express more clearly
- thematic breaks before every heading

Keep lists for real sets, steps, comparisons, or scan-heavy operational material. Keep tables when readers must compare repeated fields.

### Mechanical rhythm

Inspect uniform medium-length sentences, repeated paragraph sizes, serial one-sentence paragraphs, repeated fragments, and identical clause shapes. Vary rhythm by rebuilding the argument, not by randomly joining or splitting sentences.

### Elegant variation

Do not cycle through synonyms merely to avoid repetition. Reuse the exact name or term when it is clearest. This matters especially for technical concepts, legal actors, characters, and causal variables.

### Abstract and nominal prose

Inspect noun piles and nominalizations that hide the actor or action: `the implementation of optimization`, `the facilitation of alignment`, or `the achievement of transformation`. Name the actor and use the verb.

> Before: The implementation of automation enabled the reduction of processing time.
>
> After: The billing team automated invoice matching and cut processing time by two days.

### Promotional warmth, hedging, and emotional projection

Inspect relentlessly positive tone, unsupported superlatives, reflexive reassurance, generic empathy, and stacked hedges. Do not claim that something `deeply resonates`, `inspires communities`, or `evokes resilience` without evidence. Be warm when the relationship calls for warmth, but do not trade directness or accuracy for approval.

## Voice and meaning preservation

Preserve the writer rather than manufacturing a generic idea of a human:

- Retain dialect, regional spelling, contractions, humor, stance, and culturally meaningful politeness.
- Retain deliberate repetition, sentence fragments, punctuation habits, and specialized vocabulary when they serve the piece.
- Do not inject mistakes, anecdotes, slang, irritation, or quirky metaphors.
- Use supplied writing samples as stronger evidence of voice than this catalog.
- When no voice evidence exists, default to plain, direct language appropriate to the audience and channel.

After a rewrite, compare the source and result for changes to names, numbers, dates, chronology, quotation, causality, negation, uncertainty, confidence, actors, motives, permissions, obligations, intended consequences, and requested actions. Do not turn vague praise into a new directive or plausible-sounding fact. A cleaner sentence that changes any of these has failed.

## Final checks

1. **Direct-statement test**: Can a contrast, question, throat-clearing phrase, or dramatic frame become a direct claim?
2. **Deletion test**: Can a sentence or paragraph disappear without losing a claim, fact, transition, or necessary tone? Delete it.
3. **Subject-swap test**: Could the sentence describe almost any product, person, town, policy, or company? Replace it with specific information or cut it.
4. **Evidence test**: Does every claim of importance, consensus, causation, emotion, motive, intended use, or future effect have support and exact attribution?
5. **Purpose test**: Does each heading, list, table, bold phrase, rhetorical question, and em dash help this reader perform the task?
6. **Voice-match test**: Does the revision still sound like the source writer, including their English variety and stance?
7. **Meaning-diff test**: Did any fact, actor, relationship, uncertainty, motive, permission, obligation, or instruction change?
8. **Residue test**: Remove chatbot offers, self-evaluation, editing notes, and workflow language from reader-facing copy.

## Research basis

Concrete tics change as models and prompting systems change. The catalog therefore combines current observation with research on broader, more durable properties:

- [Wikipedia: Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia%3ASigns_of_AI_writing), live field guide last reviewed here on 2026-07-22; useful for current patterns and its warning that no single sign proves authorship
- [It's not just one thing — it's another thing](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/20/ai-writing-its-not-just-this-its-that-barrons/), TechCrunch, 2026-04-20; contemporary reporting on contrastive negation
- [The Formulaic Machine: Generative AI, Academic Writing, and the Recovery of Voice](https://albert.ias.edu/server/api/core/bitstreams/c45a10ab-d45b-48ea-a888-1107838af617/content), 2026-04-26; examples of formulaic academic constructions and recovery strategies
- [How LLMs Distort Our Written Language](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.18161), 2026-03-18; evidence that rewriting can change semantics even under narrow editing instructions
- [More Aligned, Less Diverse?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06030), 2026-05-07; evidence of reduced syntactic, lexical, and punctuation diversity in newer instruction-tuned models
- [Does AI Homogenize Student Thinking?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.21228), 2026-03-22; evidence of structural convergence and the importance of specific prompting
- [When AI Writes, Whose Voice Remains?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.22145), 2026-02-25; evidence that rewriting can erase markers of World Englishes
- [The Cost of Perfect English](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13055), 2026-05-13; evidence of pragmatic flattening in second-language writing
- [Training language models to be warm can reduce accuracy and increase sycophancy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10410-0), Nature, 2026-04-29; evidence that performed warmth can conflict with accuracy and direct disagreement
- [Writing with ChatGPT](https://openai.com/academy/writing/), OpenAI Academy, 2026-04-10; workflow guidance on audience, purpose, source material, constraints, revision, and fact checking
